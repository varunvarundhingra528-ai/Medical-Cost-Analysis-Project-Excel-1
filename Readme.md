<h2><a class="anchor" id="Business-Problem"></a>Business Problem</h2>

-The healthcare insurance industry faces challenges in managing rising medical costs and accurately pricing insurance policies.
-The key problem is to identify the factors that significantly influence medical charges, such as lifestyle habits, health conditions, and demographic characteristics.
-Without proper analysis, insurance companies may face:
-Incorrect premium pricing 
-Higher financial risk 
-Difficulty in identifying high-risk customers 
-This project aims to analyze these factors and provide insights to improve cost prediction and decision-making.

<h2><a class="anchor" id="Dataset"></a>Dataset</h2>

-Age → Age of the beneficiary 
-Sex → Gender (male/female) 
-BMI → Body Mass Index 
-Children → Number of dependents 
-Smoker → Smoking status (yes/no) 
-Region → Residential area in the US 
-Charges → Medical insurance cost 

<h2><a class="anchor" id="Basic-Statistics"></a>Basic Statistics</h2>

-Medical charges show high variability, indicating a wide range of insurance costs.
-The average BMI is above 30, indicating that most individuals fall into the overweight category.
-The dataset includes both young and middle-aged individuals with a fairly balanced distribution

<h2><a class="anchor" id="Gender-Analysis"></a>Gender Analysis</h2>

-Males show higher total medical charges; however, this may be influenced by record count rather than higher per-person costs. 
-BMI levels are similar across genders, suggesting it does not significantly explain the variation in medical expenses. 
-The difference in charges is likely driven by other factors such as smoking status, age, and lifestyle. 
-Further analysis using averages and segmented data is required to validate these findings. 

<h2><a class="anchor" id="Smoking-Impact"></a>Smoking Impact</h2>

-Smokers face average medical charges that are four times higher than those of non-smokers ($32,000 vs. $8,000).
-Choosing not to smoke saves an individual an average of $24,000 in healthcare expenses.
-The massive jump in the bar indicates that smoking moves a person from a "low-cost" medical tier to a "high-impact" financial risk category.

<h2><a class="anchor" id="Dependents-Analysis"></a>Dependents Analysis</h2>

-The correlation between the number of dependents and medical charges is 0.067998, indicating a very weak positive relationship

<h2><a class="anchor" id="Geographical-Insight"></a>Geographical Insights</h2>

-Overall, the Southeast stands out as a high-risk region in terms of both BMI and healthcare costs. 
-IT highlighting the potential impact of regional health patterns on medical expenses.
-The Southeast region has the highest average medical charges
-Other regions such as Northeast, Northwest, and Southwest show moderate variations 

<h2><a class="anchor" id="BMI-Classification"></a>BMI Classification</h2>

-A significant proportion of the population falls into the high-risk BMI categories (overweight and obese). 
-The majority of beneficiaries have BMI values above the normal range, indicating an overall unhealthy weight distribution. 
-The underweight category represents a very small portion of the population and is not a major concern in this dataset. 
-The high prevalence of elevated BMI levels suggests an increased risk of chronic diseases, which may contribute to higher medical costs

<h2><a class="anchor" id="Advance-Corrlation"></a>Advance Correlation</h2>

-The scatter plot shows a slight positive relationship between BMI and medical charges. 
-The data points are widely scattered, indicating a weak to moderate correlation. 
-This suggests that while BMI has some influence on medical costs, other factors such as smoking have a stronger impact.

<h2><a class="anchor" id="Key-Insights"></a>Key Insights</h2>

-Smoking significantly increases medical costs.
-Gender has minimal impact on medical expenses.
-Higher BMI is associated with increased healthcare costs.
-The number of dependents has a weak effect on medical charges.
-Regional differences exist but are not highly significant.

<h2><a class="anchor" id="dashboard"></a>Dashboard</h2>

-The dashboard integrates all analyses into a single, easy-to-understand interface.
-Interactive slicers provide dynamic filtering across region, smoking status, gender, and BMI categories.
-Key performance indicators at the top deliver quick and meaningful insights at a glance.
-The dashboard facilitates efficient, data-driven decision-making for stakeholders.

<h2><a class="anchor" id="Conclusion"></a>Conclusion</h2>

-Smoking is the most significant factor influencing medical charges 
-Individuals with higher BMI tend to have increased healthcare costs 
-Number of dependents has minimal impact on medical expenses 
-Regional differences exist, with some regions showing higher costs 
-Medical charges are unevenly distributed, with a few high-cost cases
